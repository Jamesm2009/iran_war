Secondary Impact Analysis
Food Security & The Global Agricultural Planting Crisis
The food security impacts extend far beyond immediate price inflation. Fertiliser shortages and surging diesel costs are causing farmers to plant less, apply less fertiliser, and switch to lower-input crops right now. These decisions show up in harvest data months later — creating a deferred wave of food insecurity that persists long after any ceasefire. The food crisis will outlast the war regardless of when it ends.
Critical Timing Risk: Northern Hemisphere planting season is underway now. Southern Hemisphere planting opens later in 2026. The full agricultural impact is not yet visible and compounds into 2027 even if the war ends immediately. This is a deferred shock that market attention will miss until harvest shortfalls materialise.

The Planting Crisis — Cascade Mechanisms
Immediate — Now

Northern Hemisphere Planting Season

IFPRI identifies immediate impacts across USA, Canada, Europe, Ukraine, China, and India. USDA confirms: corn and wheat acreage down 3% each; soy up 4%. This is not a future risk — it is happening now, with harvest consequences arriving in autumn 2026.

Deferred — Coming Months

Southern Hemisphere Planting Window

Brazil, Argentina, South Africa, and Australia face the greatest deferred exposure. Brazil’s structural dependence — 85% of fertiliser imported, ~50% via Hormuz — makes it the single most exposed major agricultural producer globally.

Yield Cascade

2026–27 Harvest Cycle at Risk

IFPRI and World Bank: reduced fertiliser application does not show up immediately — it appears at harvest. Global grain stocks will decline materially in late 2026 and early 2027, pushing food prices higher precisely when the energy shock is expected to moderate.

Energy–Food Link

Diesel Costs Compound the Squeeze

Fuel for farm machinery is a major agricultural input. With diesel prices up sharply, total farm production costs have risen significantly independent of fertiliser. Smallholders in South Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa face the hardest choices — directly driving WFP’s 45M hunger risk estimate.

IRGC Command Authority — The Core Problem
Why a Signed Deal Is Not a Working Deal
Three Facts Every Negotiator Must Understand
  • The civilian government cannot bind the IRGC. President Pezeshkian and FM Araghchi can sign documents. IRGC Commander Salami and IRGC Navy chief Tangsiri — who control Hormuz, ballistic missiles, and nuclear material — report directly to Khamenei, not to the president. A deal signed by Araghchi that Salami ignores is not a deal.
  • The IRGC confirmed it is acting offensively during the ceasefire. Pezeshkian publicly admitted the May 25 Strait attacks were offensive, not defensive — contradicting his own FM. The IRGC is not abiding by the ceasefire it nominally accepted.
  • The internet blackout proves the IRGC governs Iran. Iran's internet was shut for 88 days by IRGC decision, against the civilian government's wishes. Pezeshkian forced a partial restore (40%) via public announcement only. If the IRGC controls the internet switch, it controls the nuclear switch. Pakistan's mediation channel does not reach the decision-makers.
Military Rebuild & Oil Storage — New Intelligence
What Iran Is Doing With the Ceasefire
🔴 Missile & Drone Rebuild — Faster Than Expected

Rapid recovery: Iran restored 30 of 33 (91%) IRGC missile sites in the Strait of Hormuz within 40 days, driven by the Khatam al‑Anbiya engineering corps.

Ahead of expectations: US officials say reconstruction is outpacing intelligence projections; China is supplying equipment and Russia technical support. Pre-war capability could return within ~ 6 months.

Negotiation impact: Damage was significant but limited—key underground assets, proxy networks, and reconstitution capacity remain intact (CSIS). Each week of ceasefire strengthens Iran’s position.

Key implication: Military gains are being reversed faster than diplomatic progress, eroding expected US leverage.
🟠 Oil Storage & Well Shut-ins — A Double-Edged Sword

Storage near full: Iran’s exports (~1.7M bpd) lag production (~3.2–3.3M bpd), pushing excess into nearly maxed storage at Kharg and Jask; stocks surged rapidly in late April.

Well shut-ins underway: Columbia CGEP (May 24): Iran's crude oil wells shut-ins are manageable (historically reversible), but gas shut-ins pose greater risk—forcing rationing across power, industry, and households. Spillover shut-ins are affecting Iraq, Kuwait, and the UAE.

Restart friction: Reopening Hormuz won’t quickly normalize flows—tankers take weeks to return, and restarting wells is costly and complex; potential Gulf shut-ins could reach ~5 mb/d if disruption persists.

Key implication: Oil pressure is real but containable; gas is the true vulnerability—dampening (not removing) Iran’s incentive to concede in negotiations.
