    💡 Insight — Iran Oil Revenue +80% During the War (Hoh, Jun 9)

    Matthew Hoh's analysis: Iran's oil revenue is up 80% since the war started — the sanctioned price premium on its sales has more than offset volume losses. This means the economic mechanism that would force Iran to a deal is operating in reverse. Iran is financially better off during the war than before it. Combined with Brandon Weichert's analysis that the US Navy blockade is "leaking" — improvised, insufficient ships, Chinese vessels slipping through untouched — the bearish "Iran will buckle under blockade" thesis loses its primary mechanism. The war is structurally a stalemate Iran can outlast.

    Countries most exposed:
    🇩🇪 Germany🇬🇧 UK🇿🇦 S.Africa🇦🇺 Australia🇨🇴 Colombia🇺🇦 Ukraine

    The Strait closure is the largest single energy supply disruption in recorded history (IEA). Gulf producers have lost ~14mb/d since February 28. All existing bypass pipelines cover less than 50% of the crude gap. For LNG there is no alternative route — Qatar's Ras Laffan is offline with multi-year repair timelines confirmed by QatarEnergy.

    Refined products — the underassessed channel. IEA confirms diesel and jet fuel are more vulnerable to Hormuz disruption than crude. European diesel imports have plummeted to multi-year lows per Vortexa (Bloomberg Jun 8). The indirect exposure chain: Gulf crude → Asian refinery → finished diesel → import-dependent economy. UK (imports 2.5× its diesel production), Germany (logistics and heavy industry), and Australia (~90% fuel import dependent) all face this channel even without direct Gulf crude imports. This is a refinery grade configuration problem — Asian refineries process Gulf medium-sour grades that US light-sweet WTI cannot replicate in diesel yield. Source: Valero & Labyrinth Consulting Services.

    Hormuz toll regime — now formal Iranian state policy (Reuters/OilPrice Jun 8). Iran's envoy in Moscow confirmed the Strait will reopen WITH transit fees — $2M yuan/ship per Iranian parliamentarian source. The PSGA toll system has moved from credible-but-unverified to formal state policy. This adds permanent friction cost even after any reopening — and makes the Hormuz deal gap structurally harder to bridge, not softer. Iran's Environment Ministry is building the formal "maritime and environmental service fees" framework as permanent sovereignty infrastructure.
