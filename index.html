    💡 Insight — Iran Oil Revenue +80% During the War (Hoh, Jun 9)

    Matthew Hoh's analysis: Iran's oil revenue is up 80% since the war started — the sanctioned price premium on its sales has more than offset volume losses. This means the economic mechanism that would force Iran to a deal is operating in reverse. Iran is financially better off during the war than before it. Combined with Brandon Weichert's analysis that the US Navy blockade is "leaking" — improvised, insufficient ships, Chinese vessels slipping through untouched — the bearish "Iran will buckle under blockade" thesis loses its primary mechanism. The war is structurally a stalemate Iran can outlast.

    Countries most exposed:
    🇩🇪 Germany🇬🇧 UK🇿🇦 S.Africa🇦🇺 Australia🇨🇴 Colombia🇺🇦 Ukraine

    The Strait closure is the largest single energy supply disruption in recorded history (IEA). Gulf producers have lost ~14mb/d since February 28. All existing bypass pipelines cover less than 50% of the crude gap. For LNG there is no alternative route — Qatar's Ras Laffan is offline with multi-year repair timelines confirmed by QatarEnergy.

    Refined products — the underassessed channel. IEA confirms diesel and jet fuel are more vulnerable to Hormuz disruption than crude. European diesel imports have plummeted to multi-year lows per Vortexa (Bloomberg Jun 8). The indirect exposure chain: Gulf crude → Asian refinery → finished diesel → import-dependent economy. UK (imports 2.5× its diesel production), Germany (logistics and heavy industry), and Australia (~90% fuel import dependent) all face this channel even without direct Gulf crude imports. This is a refinery grade configuration problem — Asian refineries process Gulf medium-sour grades that US light-sweet WTI cannot replicate in diesel yield. Source: Valero & Labyrinth Consulting Services.

    Hormuz toll regime — legitimised in MOU text. MOU Article 5: toll-free for 60 days 'only.' After that, Iran-Oman negotiate 'future administration and maritime services.' Ghalibaf: Hormuz 'will never return to pre-war conditions.' This represents permanent erosion of UNCLOS free-passage norms. Goldman's '70% is the new 100%' thesis: alternative pipeline routing (Yanbu for Saudi, Fujairah/Adcop for UAE) has structurally reduced Hormuz dependency — Gulf exports may only need 70% of pre-war Hormuz flows to normalise.

    Qatar LNG — permanently 17% smaller. Iranian strikes on Ras Laffan damaged 2 full liquefaction trains — 12.8 MTPA, 17% of Qatar's pre-war 77 MTPA capacity. QatarEnergy confirmed repair timeline of 3-5 years minimum. Even at '80% restored,' the real ceiling for the rest of the decade is ~64 MTPA, not 77. This is a structural loss, not cyclical. Global LNG architecture is permanently reshaping toward US supply: 110 MTPA operating, 47 under construction, 42.5 past FID (~200 MTPA in pipeline, 2.5x Qatar's pre-war capacity). Japan signing 27-year US-linked deals, Germany locking 20+ year US/Canadian volumes, India taking 40% of needs from US (8x pre-war), South Africa's first LNG import terminal via Exxon at Richards Bay. The buyers are voting with 20-year signatures — this doesn't reverse when the news cycle moves on. Affects UK, Germany, Australia most directly.

    Oil market — headline vs physical divergence. Brent ~$78, WTI ~$75. US markets closed Jun 19 (Juneteenth). Kpler: 25 Hormuz crossings Jun 18 — highest since April but still 70% below pre-war 84/day. Evenly split inbound/outbound; 5 sanctioned vessels among crossings. 10 Iranian supertankers (20M bbl) sailing Chabahar→China. 80 confirmed mines in shipping lane (AGBI). Sell-side: Citi $75 Q3; Goldman $80 Q4; Morgan Stanley 50% back Sep, 80% Dec. Jeff Currie: flows normalise end of 2026.

    Shipping costs locking in — Jul 1 inflection. Maersk: $500M/month in extra fuel costs from Hormuz disruption. Hapag-Lloyd: $58-70M/week. Both rolling accrued crisis expenses into annual cargo contracts from July 1. This is the critical detail: even if oil stays lower, the higher bunker costs from the crisis are being locked into 12-month shipping rates. The inflation from Hormuz doesn't disappear when prices fall — it gets baked in. Every import-dependent country in the briefing faces a permanently repriced shipping cost baseline for the next year.
