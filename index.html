    Crude Oil Grades — Why US Supply Cannot Simply Replace Gulf Supply
    API Gravity vs Sulfur content · Middle Eastern oil in the refinery "sweet spot" · US crude is not · Source: Valero & Labyrinth Consulting Services
    Asian refineries are configured for Gulf medium-sour grades (Basrah, Arab Medium, 30–40 API / 2–2.5% sulfur). US WTI is light-sweet (50–55 API, <0.5% sulfur) — wrong grade for diesel-optimised configurations in Korea, Japan, Singapore, and India. Volume substitution is possible; diesel yield per barrel is not. The UK, Australia, and Germany diesel shortage is a refinery configuration problem, not just a price problem.

    The Strait of Hormuz closure is the largest single energy supply disruption in recorded history (IEA). All existing bypass pipelines combined cover less than 50% of the crude gap. For LNG there is no alternative route — Qatar's Ras Laffan is offline for an estimated 3–5 years.

    UAE — the hidden third coalition member. The Wall Street Journal (May 29) revealed that the UAE carried out dozens of airstrikes against Iran from Day 1 of the conflict, including strikes on the Asaluyeh petrochemical complex — a major source of LNG and refined products for Asian markets. The UAE absorbed over 2,800 missiles and drones in response; $120bn has been wiped from UAE market capitalisation. Saudi Arabia-UAE tensions are now visible: Riyadh privately complained that Emirati strikes risked triggering Iranian retaliation against regional energy facilities. The Gulf coalition is fractured, complicating any regional settlement.

    Refined products — the underassessed channel. The original briefing focused primarily on crude oil. The IEA's March and April reports confirm that diesel and jet fuel are more vulnerable than crude to the Hormuz disruption. Gulf producers exported 3.3mb/d of refined products in 2025 — now largely offline. European diesel futures hit $200/bbl (second-highest on record). Singapore middle distillate prices reached all-time highs above $290/bbl. Asian refineries have cut runs by ~6mb/d as feedstock tightens — directly affecting countries like Australia that source refined diesel from Asian refineries processing Gulf crude.

    The indirect exposure mechanism. Countries that don't import Gulf crude directly are still exposed through a two-step chain: Gulf crude → Asian refinery → finished diesel → import-dependent economy. Australia (~50% of diesel indirectly exposed), UK (imports 2.5x its diesel production), and Germany (logistics and heavy industry diesel) all face this indirect channel. South Africa has responded with active diesel subsidies costing R17.2B — exceeding its contingency reserve.

    The IRGC's mine-gauntlet doctrine means the Strait cannot be safely reopened by political declaration alone. Physical mine-clearing takes months. Markets pricing in instant supply restoration upon a deal announcement are pricing in a mechanism that does not physically exist — for either crude or refined products.

    🌾 Food Security & Agriculture
    Unchanged — May 28
    TL;DR
    The food crisis will outlast the war regardless of when it ends. Planting decisions made now determine harvests in autumn 2026 and into 2027. ~33% of global fertiliser transits Hormuz. The deferred harvest cascade is the most underappreciated risk in this briefing.
    Countries most exposed:
    🇿🇦 S.Africa🇨🇴 Colombia🇺🇦 Ukraine🇬🇧 UK🇩🇪 Germany
    Scenario linkage:
    All scenarios — deferred shock persists regardless

    ~33% of global fertiliser trade normally transits the Strait. Urea prices surged +19% to $590/MT, while grain prices remained low — a severe margin squeeze. The rational farmer response — planting less, applying less fertiliser, switching crops — is already in USDA data: US corn and wheat acreage down 3% each; soy up 4%.

    The critical dynamic: reduced fertiliser application shows up 4–6 months later at harvest. The Northern Hemisphere planting season is underway now. Southern Hemisphere (Brazil, Argentina, South Africa, Australia) opens later in 2026. Global grain stocks will decline materially in late 2026 and into 2027 — a second inflation wave arriving precisely when the energy shock moderates.

    Brazil: 85% fertiliser imported, ~50% via Hormuz — single most structurally exposed major agricultural producer globally. India's Kharif season (June–July) faces genuine phosphate shortage risk. WFP estimates +45 million at acute hunger risk if war persists past mid-2026 with oil above $100.

    📈 Financial Stress & Sovereign Yields
    ↑ Assessment 6
    TL;DR
    No major central bank can cut rates (inflation) or raise them (recession) cleanly. The stagflation trap is global and confirmed. US 30-yr peaked at 5.19%; $9.7T in US debt matures in 2026. The war is now a financial crisis as much as an energy crisis.
    Countries most exposed:
    🇺🇸 USA🇬🇧 UK🇩🇪 Germany🇨🇴 Colombia🇿🇦 S.Africa
    Scenario linkage:
    Sc3 — bond vigilante riskSc2 — ongoing pressure

    Sovereign yield stress is the most globally synchronised aspect of this conflict. US 30-yr peaked 5.19% (May 19); 10-yr peaked 4.69% — just 31 basis points from the psychological 5% threshold that would force the Fed's hand regardless of growth. UK gilts peaked at 5.2% (May 18). Japan 10-yr at 2.81% — a multi-decade high. A single global transmission: energy inflation → consumer price inflation → central bank paralysis → sovereign risk re-pricing.

    The $9.7T problem: The US must refinance $9.7T in maturing debt during 2026 at structurally higher rates. This creates a compounding fiscal cost that worsens regardless of whether a deal is struck. The war has permanently reset the US fiscal position for the next 3–5 years.

    The stagflation trap is most acute for the UK and Germany. Under Scenario 3, Germany faces near-certain deep recession. UK real household incomes are declining faster than during the 2022 energy crisis. Colombia and South Africa carry significant USD-denominated debt — dollar strength plus higher US rates creates an external debt servicing crisis risk on top of existing energy and food pressures.
